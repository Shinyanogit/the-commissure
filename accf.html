Overview
Anterior cervical corpectomy and fusion (ACCF) is a surgical procedure that relieves pressure on the spinal cord by removing one or more intervertebral discs and the vertebral body. The procedure consists of three major steps: cervical discectomy and corpectomy, vertebral cage implantation, and anterior spinal fixation. By replacing the resected vertebral body with an artificial cage, ACCF provides effective decompression and stabilization, making it a valuable surgical technique for treating cervical spinal disorders that extend posteriorly to the vertebral body.
ACCF is commonly performed to treat cervical disorders in which the pathological lesion is located behind the vertebral body and cannot be adequately addressed by discectomy alone. Common indications include:
  • Cervical spondylotic myelopathy
  • Ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL)
  • Cervical spinal trauma and vertebral fractures
  • Tumors involving the vertebral body
In this illustration, a herniated cervical intervertebral disc is shown compressing the spinal cord. Cervical disc herniation may produce a variety of neurological symptoms, including pain, numbness, tingling, and weakness in the upper and lower extremities.
This sagittal view illustrates a cervical intervertebral disc herniation. As shown, a herniated disc may migrate behind the vertebral body and cause extensive compression of the spinal cord. Such a lesion can be difficult to remove through discectomy alone because a substantial portion of the herniated disc is located posterior to the vertebral body. In this situation, ACCF may be preferred over anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF), as removal of the vertebral body provides direct access to the migrated disc fragment and allows for more complete decompression of the spinal cord.
The initial step of ACCF is cervical discectomy, during which the affected intervertebral disc is removed. In some cases, however, the herniated disc cannot be completely excised because disc fragments remain behind the vertebral body. To access these residual fragments directly, a corpectomy is then performed to remove the vertebral body. This provides a clear surgical corridor to the posterior aspect of the vertebral body, allowing complete removal of the remaining disc fragments and adequate decompression of the spinal cord.
Once adequate decompression has been confirmed, a vertebral body replacement device is implanted to restore spinal stability. Several reconstruction options are available, including:
  • Cadaveric bone grafts (allografts)
  • Autografts (e.g., iliac crest bone grafts)
  • Expandable vertebral body cages
In this illustration, an expandable cage is inserted to replace the resected vertebral body. Once in place, the cage is expanded to restore the height of the spinal column and achieve a secure fit between the adjacent vertebrae. Many expandable cages are designed with openings that allow bone to grow into and around the implant, thereby promoting solid spinal fusion.
To stabilize the reconstructed spine, anterior fixation is performed using a cervical plate and screws. This instrumentation secures the vertebral body replacement, prevents implant migration, and provides immediate mechanical stability while spinal fusion occurs. Because the fused vertebral segments no longer move independently, cervical range of motion may be reduced, particularly when multiple levels undergo discectomy and corpectomy. After fixation, surgeons typically use radiographic imaging to confirm the correct placement of the plate, screws, and vertebral body replacement device.
Some of the key features of ACCF are as follows:
  • It is highly effective for treating lesions located behind the vertebral body.
  • It consists of three major steps: discectomy and corpectomy, expandable cage implantation, and cervical plate fixation.
  • Several options are available for reconstructing the resected vertebral body, including cadaveric bone grafts, autografts, and expandable vertebral body cages.
  • Fusion may reduce cervical range of motion, particularly when multiple levels are involved.
Despite its greater complexity and invasiveness, ACCF remains an effective surgical approach for treating cervical spinal disorders that cannot be adequately managed with discectomy and fusion alone.
