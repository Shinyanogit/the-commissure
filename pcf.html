Posterior Cervical Foraminotomy (PCF)
Indication for PCF
Laminoforaminotomy
Microdiscectomy
Preserved Motion after PCF
Overview
Posterior cervical foraminotomy (PCF) is a spinal surgical technique that relieves pressure on nerves of the neck by removing a small amount of bone from the spine. Compared with some other neck surgeries, a major advantage of PCF is that it allows patients to maintain normal neck motion even after the surgery. Surgeons may additionally perform surgeries to remove parts of the herniated spinal discs to achieve adequate neural decompression and symptom relief.
PCF is commonly performed to treat conditions that affect the nerves as they exit the spine of the neck. Examples of conditions that may be treated with PCF are as follows:
  • Cervical spondylotic radiculopathy
  • Lateral cervical disc herniation
  • Foraminal stenosis
Here, a lateral cervical disc herniation at the C5 level is depicted. In this case, the herniated disc is compressing the part of the spinal nerve as it exits the spine. Such a condition can cause a variety of symptoms, such as a tingling sensation or pain in the arm, weakness of the arm and hand. In severe cases, if the disc is compressing the spinal cord, patients may have trouble with balance, walking, or hand coordination.
During PCF, surgeons first identify the precise location where a small portion of bone will be removed, using medical imaging and anatomical landmarks. The surgeons then carefully remove parts of the bone near the area where the nerve exits the spine, a procedure known as laminoforaminotomy. This creates more space around the nerve to shift outwards and escape from the pressing disc. In some cases, creating this opening allows surgeons to directly observe the herniated disc that is pressing the nerve.
When necessary, surgeons may further perform a discectomy, which involves the removal of the herniated disc, to ensure that the nerve is fully decompressed. To perform this step safely and accurately, surgeons may use special types of microscopes during disc removal, a procedure known as microdiscectomy.
One of the advantages of performing PCF is that it allows patients to maintain normal neck motion even after the surgery. Unlike some other types of neck surgeries, such as posterior cervical discectomy (PCDF) and anterior cervical discectomy (ACDF), PCF does not require the spine to be permanently fused. However, PCF may not be suitable for conditions that require surgical interventions on multiple spine levels. Performing PCF on multiple levels may lead to spinal instability, which could in turn lead to worsening of symptoms.
Some of the key features of PCF are as follows:
  • It is a procedure where a small portion of bone is removed from the spine to create more space around the affected nerve.
  • It is effective in treating conditions that cause compression of the nerve as it exits the spine.
  • Surgeons may additionally perform discectomy to ensure adequate pressure relief.
  • It preserves the neck motion of the patients but may not be suitable for patients with extensive spinal disease.
Due to its ability to maintain motion, PCF may be considered an alternative to fusion surgeries for selected patients with cervical compression.
